A village bank runs on trust — not credit scores.

Join eight neighbors around a shared table. Over six monthly meetings, you'll contribute to a common pool, vote on who gets loans, weather emergencies, and build (or break) trust with the group. This kind of group is called a chama in Kenya, a tanda in Mexico, susu in Ghana, hui in China. Tens of millions of people bank this way — especially where banks won't go.

The real lesson of community banking

Group savings clubs run on trust, not collateral. You didn't need a credit score, a bank account, or ID verification. You needed eight people willing to show up every month and vouch for each other.

Tens of millions of people bank this way. In Kenya they call it a chama, in Mexico a tanda, in Ghana a susu, in China a hui, in Ethiopia an ekub. Same idea — pool the money, lend it out, share the profit. Older than banks, and still going strong where banks won't reach.
