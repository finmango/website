Understand and maximize your employee benefits package

So you just got a job offer? Congrats!! üéâ

If you're at all confused about some of the benefits your new job may come with (or simply wish to brush up) then you've come to the right place!

Employee benefits can add significant value to your compensation package‚Äîsometimes worth 30-40% of your base salary. Understanding these benefits helps you make informed career decisions and maximize your total compensation.

1. Retirement Benefits (401(k), 403(b), Pension)

‚ñº
401(k) Plans

A 401(k) is a retirement savings account that lets you contribute pre-tax dollars from your paycheck. Your contributions grow tax-deferred until retirement, and many employers offer matching contributions.

üí∞ If your employer offers a match (like 50% of up to 6% of salary), ALWAYS contribute enough to get the full match. It's literally free money!

Contribution Limits (2025): You can contribute up to $23,000 per year if you're under 50, or $30,500 if you're 50 or older.

Vesting: Your contributions are always yours, but employer match funds may require you to stay at the company for a certain period (typically 3-6 years) before they're fully "vested" (yours to keep).

Roth 401(k)

Some employers offer a Roth 401(k) option alongside traditional 401(k). You contribute after-tax dollars, but withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.

When to choose Roth: If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement than you are now, Roth makes sense. This is often true for younger workers early in their careers.

403(b) Plans

Similar to 401(k) but offered by non-profit organizations, schools, and certain government entities. Same contribution limits and tax advantages as 401(k) plans.

Pension Plans

Less common today, but some employers (especially government and unions) offer defined benefit pension plans that pay you a guaranteed monthly income in retirement based on your salary and years of service.

Pensions are extremely valuable‚Äîif your employer offers one, factor this heavily into your compensation evaluation.

2. Health Insurance & Healthcare Benefits

‚ñº
Medical Insurance

Employers typically cover a portion of your health insurance premiums. Key terms to understand:

Premium: The monthly cost of your insurance (often split between you and employer)

Deductible: Amount you pay before insurance kicks in (typically $500-$3,000)

Copay: Fixed amount you pay for doctor visits or prescriptions

Out-of-pocket maximum: Most you'll pay in a year before insurance covers 100%

üè• Compare the total cost (premiums + expected out-of-pocket) across plans, not just the premium. A low-premium plan with high deductible may cost more overall if you use healthcare regularly.
HSA (Health Savings Account)

If you have a high-deductible health plan, you can contribute to an HSA with triple tax benefits:

1. Contributions are tax-deductible
2. Growth is tax-free
3. Withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free

2025 Limits: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family

üí° Pro tip: HSAs roll over year to year (unlike FSAs) and can be invested like a 401(k). Many people use HSAs as a "stealth IRA" for retirement savings.
FSA (Flexible Spending Account)

Lets you set aside pre-tax dollars for healthcare expenses. Unlike HSA, FSA funds are "use it or lose it" each year (with some exceptions for small carryovers).

Good for predictable medical expenses like glasses, prescriptions, or planned procedures.

Dental & Vision Insurance

Often separate from medical insurance. Dental typically covers cleanings, X-rays, and a percentage of major work. Vision covers eye exams and glasses/contacts.

These are usually inexpensive and worth enrolling in if offered.

3. Stock & Equity Compensation

‚ñº
Stock Options (ISOs & NSOs)

Stock options give you the right to buy company stock at a set price (strike price) in the future. If the company's stock price goes up, you can buy at the lower strike price and profit from the difference.

Vesting Schedule: Typically vest over 4 years with a 1-year "cliff" (meaning you get nothing if you leave before 1 year, then get 25% at 1 year and monthly/quarterly amounts after).

‚ö†Ô∏è Stock options can be worth a lot or nothing‚Äîthey're valuable only if the company's stock price rises above your strike price. Don't count on them as guaranteed compensation.
RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)

RSUs are actual shares of company stock granted to you, but you can't sell them until they vest. Once vested, they're yours to keep or sell.

RSUs are taxed as regular income when they vest, so plan for the tax hit. Some companies withhold shares to cover taxes automatically.

üìà RSUs are generally better than options because they have value even if the stock price drops (though less value). Options are worth $0 if the stock price is below strike price.
ESPP (Employee Stock Purchase Plan)

Lets you buy company stock at a discount (typically 15%) through payroll deductions. Often you can buy at the lower price between the start and end of a purchase period.

This is essentially free money if you sell immediately after purchase‚Äîinstant 15% return (minus taxes).

Profit Sharing & Bonuses

Some companies offer profit-sharing plans or performance bonuses. These can be cash, stock, or contributions to your retirement account.

Always ask about the typical payout percentage and what metrics determine it‚Äîbonuses listed as "up to X%" are often much lower in reality.

4. Time Off & Work-Life Benefits

‚ñº
PTO (Paid Time Off)

Most companies offer 10-25 days per year combining vacation and sick leave. Some separate them into vacation days, sick days, and personal days.

Unlimited PTO: Some companies offer "unlimited" vacation, but studies show employees often take less time off under these policies. Always ask about the typical/expected amount people actually take.

üå¥ PTO is part of your compensation‚Äîuse it! Unused vacation time is often forfeited when you leave a company (unless required by state law to pay out).
Holidays

Typical companies offer 6-10 paid holidays per year (New Year's, Memorial Day, July 4th, Labor Day, Thanksgiving, Christmas, etc.).

Some companies close entirely between Christmas and New Year's, giving everyone extra time off.

Parental Leave

Paid parental leave varies widely‚Äîfrom nothing to 6+ months. Some companies offer equal leave for all parents, while others differentiate between birthing and non-birthing parents.

This is a crucial benefit if you plan to have children. Also check if adoption leave is covered.

Remote Work & Flexibility

Remote/hybrid work policies, flexible hours, and work-from-anywhere options have significant value even if they're not dollar amounts.

Consider: commute time saved = more life hours, flexibility for family/personal needs, potential to live in lower-cost areas.

Sabbaticals

Some companies offer extended paid or unpaid sabbaticals (1-3 months) after a certain number of years of service.

This is a rare but valuable perk for long-term planning, travel, or personal projects.

5. Additional Benefits & Perks

‚ñº
Life Insurance

Many employers provide free basic life insurance (often 1x your annual salary) and allow you to purchase additional coverage at group rates.

If you have dependents, consider purchasing supplemental coverage through your employer‚Äîit's usually cheaper than individual policies.

Disability Insurance

Short-term disability (STD): Covers portion of salary (typically 60-70%) for 3-6 months if you can't work due to injury/illness.

Long-term disability (LTD): Kicks in after short-term ends and can last until retirement age if needed.

üõ°Ô∏è Disability insurance is often overlooked but extremely important‚Äîyou're far more likely to become disabled during your career than to die while working.
Tuition Reimbursement

Many companies reimburse tuition for job-related courses, degrees, or certifications (typically $5,000-$10,000 per year).

Some require you to stay with the company for a period after completing the education or you'll need to pay it back.

Professional Development

Budget for conferences, training, certifications, books, or online courses. Some companies are generous (unlimited within reason), others provide fixed annual amounts.

This is valuable for career growth‚Äîprioritize companies that invest in employee development.

Commuter Benefits

Pre-tax accounts for public transit or parking expenses. The IRS allows up to $315/month (2025) to be set aside tax-free.

This effectively gives you a 20-30% discount on commuting costs depending on your tax bracket.

Gym Memberships & Wellness

Free gym access, fitness class reimbursement, wellness stipends, or on-site facilities. Some companies offer incentives for meeting health goals.

Student Loan Repayment Assistance

Growing benefit where employers contribute to your student loan payments. Companies can contribute up to $5,250 per year tax-free.

This is extremely valuable if you have significant student debt.

Relocation Assistance

If moving for a job, companies may offer: moving expense reimbursement, temporary housing, house-hunting trips, or cash bonuses.

Always negotiate this separately‚Äîrelocation can cost $10,000-$30,000 out of pocket.

Food & Meals

Free meals (breakfast/lunch/dinner), snacks, or meal stipends. This can save $200-$500/month in some cases, especially at tech companies.

Child Care

On-site childcare, childcare stipends, or backup care for emergencies. Can save thousands per month in childcare costs.

Dependent Care FSA allows up to $5,000/year pre-tax for childcare expenses.
